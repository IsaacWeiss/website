The inclusive classroom

Diversity and inclusion: two concepts of great concern to the field of education. Teachers must be prepared to respond to diverse students, and make sure their classes are not accidentally excluding anyone. There is a growing awareness that it is problematic to treat all students identically. But viewing students as members of identity groups ("Students of Background X Need Y") further buries the hard truth of diversity: every individual is different.

There are no universally applicable rules about how to effectively reach students. Not one. (Except perhaps when it comes to general qualities the teacher should have: the work ethic to move mountains on students' behalf, sufficient passion for the subject matter to make it interesting to anyone, etc.) And the belief that there are is harmful, not helpful—the line between preconception and prejudice is thin.

So, how should a professional teacher handle diversity, if not by treating students according to type?

The real key is empathy.

It is an uphill battle. The typical school environment is not conducive to teachers treating students as individuals. The large groups of homogenous ages, the regimented schedules and seating arrangementsâ€”even the words we use, like "classes" and "grades," betray an inescapable worldview in which people (students are better understood as people) can be categorized. And, once categorized, it follows that if a teacher understands a category he or she understands all students within it.

This is bad. Dehumanizing, in fact.

People are diverse.

I believe that nothing can be assumedâ€”not even on the basis of age! Any set of guidelines for dealing with diversity will itself likely elide the diversity within the categories it discusses. All a teacher can do—and what great teachers will do—is be empathetic and work to understand, because every individual is unique.

Following are anecdotes about some unique situations I have encountered, illustrative of the diversity which exists in music education and the need for empathy it creates.

A traumatized student

Six weeks into my second semester of student teaching, the 7th grade Concert Orchestra was joined by a student who had just transferred to the district. A few days later, he was rushed to the hospital and received a five-day suspension for drinking alcohol in school to the point of incapacitation. This took me and my cooperating teacher completely by surprise. We knew only that he was a foster child and had had some problems at a previous home. I had spent a couple of days working with him one-on-one to catch him up with the rest of the class, and my impression was that he was quiet and bright, a fast learner. My cooperating teacher shared my impression. Clearly that picture had been incomplete.

The timing was unfortunate; we only had him for three block schedule rotations after that before in-personal classes were suspended again. He spent one of those days talking with my cooperating teacher in his office while I taught, and the other talkingâ€”but mostly workingâ€”with me while my cooperating teacher taught. In short, it appears he had a history of abuse and neglect, and came from a very bad environment prior to his abrupt change of location. He got drunk, in an attempt to get high, in an attempt to alleviate symptoms of depression. He was twelve.

I never fully appreciated the meaning of "creating a safe environment" until I found myself asking a twelve-year-old about his withdrawal symptoms. I found myself in something like the role of a counselor—we talked about life goals and what it means to be a man. When I last saw him, he was at a crossroads in his life; he had the chance to make a fresh start. It is not easy to turn one's life around all in an instant, but I hope he succeeds.

Being the outsider

I have personal experience on the other side of the diversity question. A significant share of extant choral repertoire is directly transferred from Christian religious services. In my teens, as a homeschooler, I took high school classes in band, orchestra, music technology, and music theory, but never choir. I only asked the choral director once; while he expressed sympathy, to him it was absolutely necessary that his public school choir class should be taught Christian religious music. Hearing this as an Orthodox Jew, I determined that if choir meant singing Christian texts, then choir was closed to me.

As a college music student, more resolute, I negotiated that I would sing open vowels without consonants on the religious words (e.g, "ah-oo ey-ee" while others sung "agnus dei"). For the concert, thankfully not performed in a church, I went bareheaded in order not to give the appearance of a Jew singing praise to Jesus. Still, that choir was an unhappy experience, and I had no desire to pursue it past one semester.

Everyone should feel welcome in any music class. If accomodations can possibly be made, the teacher has the ethical responsibility to make them. Excluding someone who would enjoy participating is not an option for me as a teacher. Nor should any student feel the need to voluntarily undergo erasure. Whatever accomodations a student needs, that student deserves, and I will make it work. End of story.
